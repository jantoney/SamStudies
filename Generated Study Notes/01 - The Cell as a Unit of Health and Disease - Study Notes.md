## 1. Genome and Epigenome

The human genome contains about 3.2 billion base pairs, but only about 20,000 protein-coding genes, which account for about 1.5% of the genome. More than 85% of the genome is transcribed, and almost 80% is devoted to regulating gene expression, so many disease-associated variants sit in non-protein-coding DNA rather than in exons. [1][2]

### Core ideas

- Noncoding DNA is not "junk"; it contains promoters, enhancers, chromatin-organizing regions, regulatory RNAs, mobile genetic elements, and structural regions such as telomeres and centromeres. [2]
- Humans are more than 99.5% DNA-identical, so clinically important variation is encoded in less than 0.5% of the genome. [2]
- Common forms of variation are SNPs and CNVs. SNPs are usually biallelic single-base variants; CNVs are larger gains, losses, or rearrangements of contiguous DNA. [2][3]
- SNPs can be neutral, directly functional, or useful as markers through linkage disequilibrium. Most have weak effects individually. [2][3]
- Epigenetics means heritable changes in gene expression that are not caused by altered DNA sequence. This explains why cells with the same genome can have different phenotypes. [3]

### High-yield distinctions

| Concept | Key point | Exam angle |
|---|---|---|
| SNP | Single nucleotide change, usually biallelic | May alter susceptibility or serve as a linked marker |
| CNV | Large duplicated/deleted/rearranged DNA segment | Can affect coding sequence dosage and phenotype |
| Euchromatin | Dispersed, transcriptionally active | Open chromatin -> active gene expression |
| Heterochromatin | Dense, transcriptionally inactive | Condensed chromatin -> gene silencing |
| Epigenetic change | Alters expression without changing sequence | Explains cell-specific phenotype from same genome |

High-yield distinctions in this table are drawn from the genome and chromatin sections. [2][3][4]

### Chromatin organization

Genomic DNA is packaged into nucleosomes, each made of about 147 base pairs of DNA wrapped around a histone octamer, with linker histone H1 helping stabilize higher-order structure. Only euchromatin is transcriptionally permissive; heterochromatin is condensed and inactive. [3][4]

- DNA methylation of regulatory regions usually promotes chromatin condensation and transcriptional silencing. [4]
- Histone acetylation generally opens chromatin and increases transcription. [3][4]
- Histone methylation can either activate or repress transcription depending on the residue that is modified. [4]
- "Writers," "erasers," "readers," and chromatin-remodeling complexes dynamically regulate chromatin state. [4]

### Noncoding RNAs

MicroRNAs are short RNAs, about 22 nucleotides long on average, that regulate gene expression post-transcriptionally. Pri-miRNA is processed to mature miRNA, Dicer helps generate the mature form, and the RNA-induced silencing complex (RISC) then uses base pairing to cleave target mRNA or repress translation. [4][5]

Long noncoding RNAs are more than 200 nucleotides long and regulate gene expression in several ways, including chromatin binding, transcription-factor sequestration, chromatin modification, and scaffolding of protein complexes. XIST is the classic example because it mediates X-chromosome inactivation. [4][5]

| miRNA | lncRNA |
|---|---|
| Short | Long (>200 nt) |
| Main effect: post-transcriptional silencing | Main effect: transcriptional and chromatin regulation |
| Uses Dicer and RISC | Often acts by chromatin binding or scaffolding |
| Cleaves mRNA or represses translation | Can activate or repress transcription |

This comparison is based on the ncRNA section and Figures 1.3 to 1.4. [4][5]

### Gene editing

CRISPR/Cas9 uses a guide RNA to direct Cas9 to a homologous DNA sequence, where Cas9 creates a double-strand break. Repair by nonhomologous end joining (NHEJ) tends to introduce disruptive insertions or deletions, whereas homologous recombination/repair (HDR) can introduce a precise sequence if donor DNA is supplied. [5][6]

## 2. Cellular Housekeeping, Membranes, and Organelles

Cell viability depends on compartmentalized functions within membrane-bound organelles. Compartmentalization protects the cell from its own degradative enzymes and allows organelle-specific environments such as low pH or high calcium. [6][7]

### Organelles at a glance

| Organelle / compartment | Main job | High-yield association |
|---|---|---|
| Cytosol | Metabolism, transport, protein translation | Major site for free ribosome protein synthesis |
| Mitochondria | ATP generation, apoptosis, metabolic intermediates | Maternal inheritance; cytochrome c release |
| Rough ER | Synthesis/folding of membrane and secreted proteins | Unfolded protein response |
| Smooth ER | Lipid/steroid synthesis, detoxification, calcium handling | Drug metabolism; sarcoplasmic reticulum |
| Golgi | Protein modification, sorting, trafficking | Mannose-6-phosphate targeting to lysosomes |
| Endosomes | Transport and sorting of internalized material | Early -> late endosome maturation |
| Lysosomes | Acid hydrolase-driven degradation | Heterophagy, autophagy, phagolysosomes |
| Peroxisomes | Oxidative metabolism, very long-chain fatty acids | Catalase/peroxidase-rich |

This organelle summary is synthesized from the housekeeping overview and Figure 1.6. [6][7]

### Plasma membrane

The plasma membrane is a fluid phospholipid bilayer with asymmetric lipid distribution. Phosphatidylinositol on the inner leaflet participates in second-messenger signaling, phosphatidylserine normally stays on the inner leaflet but flips outward during apoptosis, and glycolipids/sphingomyelin are enriched on the outer leaflet where they help mediate cell-cell and cell-matrix interactions. [8]

Lipid rafts are membrane microdomains that enrich selected lipids and proteins and therefore influence signaling, adhesion, endocytosis, and membrane specialization. [8]

### Membrane transport

Small nonpolar molecules cross membranes easily, but larger polar molecules and ions require specific transporters. Channel proteins form hydrophilic pores and allow rapid movement down gradients, whereas carrier proteins bind a solute and move it more slowly through conformational change; active transport requires energy from ATP or an ion gradient. [9][10]

Because intracellular macromolecules draw water inward osmotically, cells must continuously pump out inorganic ions to avoid swelling. ATP depletion therefore causes failure of ion pumps, osmotic swelling, and eventually membrane rupture. [9]

| Channel | Carrier |
|---|---|
| Fast | Slower |
| Forms pore | Binds ligand and changes conformation |
| Usually moves down electrochemical gradient | Can mediate passive or active transport |
| Typical use: ions, very small solutes | Typical use: glucose and other specific metabolites |

The channel-carrier distinction comes from the membrane transport section and Figure 1.8. [9][10]

### Endocytosis and exocytosis

- Caveolae-mediated endocytosis uses caveolin and is important in potocytosis, signaling, and some specific uptake processes. [10]
- Clathrin-mediated pinocytosis and receptor-mediated endocytosis internalize extracellular fluid and receptor-ligand complexes. [9][10]
- LDL and transferrin are classic receptor-mediated endocytosis examples; ligand release occurs in the acidic endosome, and receptors recycle back to the surface. [10][11]
- Transcytosis moves endocytosed material from one side of a cell to the other. [10]
- Exocytosis releases vesicle contents outside the cell. [10]

### Cytoskeleton

The cytoskeleton controls cell shape, polarity, intracellular organization, vesicle movement, and cell motility. [7][11]

| Component | Main role | High-yield examples |
|---|---|---|
| Actin microfilaments | Cell shape and movement | Myosin-based contraction; cortical network |
| Intermediate filaments | Tensile strength | Vimentin, desmin, neurofilaments, GFAP, cytokeratins |
| Microtubules | Organelle transport, mitotic spindle, cilia | Kinesin anterograde, dynein retrograde |

Intermediate filaments are especially useful in pathology because their tissue-specific expression helps identify poorly differentiated tumors. [11]

### Cell junctions

- Tight junctions seal adjacent cells and preserve apical-basolateral polarity. [12]
- Desmosomes provide mechanical cell-cell attachment; hemidesmosomes attach cells to ECM. [11][12]
- Cadherins mediate many cell-cell anchoring junctions, while integrins mediate many cell-ECM attachments. [12]
- Gap junctions are made of connexons and allow passage of ions and small molecules between adjacent cells; in cardiac muscle they help coordinate contraction. [12]

### ER and Golgi

Rough ER synthesizes membrane and secreted proteins, supports folding and disulfide-bond formation, and begins N-linked glycosylation. If proteins fail to fold correctly they are retained and degraded; if misfolded proteins accumulate excessively, ER stress triggers the unfolded protein response and can end in apoptosis. [12][14]

The common cystic fibrosis mutation causes CFTR misfolding, ER retention, and degradation, illustrating how a trafficking defect can produce disease even when a protein is synthesized. [12]

The Golgi modifies proteins and lipids from cis to trans, adds O-linked oligosaccharides, recycles proteins back to ER, and dispatches cargo to the plasma membrane, lysosomes, or secretory vesicles. Mannose-6-phosphate tagging is the key signal that directs enzymes to lysosomes. [12][13]

Smooth ER is prominent in steroid-producing tissues, in detoxification pathways such as hepatic cytochrome P-450 systems, and in calcium storage, including the sarcoplasmic reticulum of muscle. [13]

### Waste disposal

Lysosomes contain acid hydrolases and degrade material delivered by endocytosis, phagocytosis, and autophagy. Proteasomes degrade ubiquitin-tagged cytosolic proteins, including denatured proteins and short-lived regulatory proteins. [13][14]

| Pathway | Main substrate | Key mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Lysosome | Endocytosed material, phagocytosed particles, worn-out organelles | Acid hydrolases in low pH compartment |
| Autophagy | Senescent organelles / protein aggregates | Autophagosome fuses with lysosome |
| Proteasome | Ubiquitinated cytosolic proteins | Multi-subunit protease complex |
| Phagolysosome | Microbes / debris in professional phagocytes | Phagosome + lysosome fusion |

This catabolism table summarizes Figure 1.10 and the surrounding text. [13][14]

### Mitochondria and metabolism

Mitochondria have their own circular genome, encode a small subset of cellular proteins, and are inherited almost entirely maternally because the ovum contributes nearly all cytoplasmic organelles to the zygote. Their proteins, however, come from both mitochondrial and nuclear genes. [13][14]

Mitochondria generate ATP by oxidative phosphorylation, supply metabolic intermediates for biosynthesis, help synthesize selected macromolecules such as heme, and participate directly in cell death pathways. [7][14][15]

In rapidly growing cells, metabolism shifts toward increased glucose and glutamine uptake, increased glycolysis, and reduced reliance on oxidative phosphorylation for ATP production. This Warburg effect preserves carbon skeletons for biosynthesis rather than fully oxidizing them. [15]

Mitochondria also regulate life-death decisions: mitochondrial outer membrane permeabilization allows cytochrome c release and apoptosis, while severe injury can open membrane permeability transition pores and contribute to necrotic cell death. [15]

## 3. Cellular Activation, Signaling, and Growth Factors

Cells constantly integrate inputs from pathogens, damaged neighbors, direct cell contacts, ECM, cytokines, hormones, and growth factors. Signaling may be paracrine, autocrine, synaptic, or endocrine depending on the spatial relationship between the signal source and target cell. [16]

### Signaling modes and receptors

Intracellular receptors respond to lipid-soluble ligands, such as steroid hormones and vitamin D, that cross the membrane and regulate transcription directly. Nitric oxide is a special diffusible signal that activates guanylyl cyclase and cGMP signaling in adjacent vascular smooth muscle cells. [16]

Cell-surface receptors usually signal by opening ion channels, activating G proteins, activating intrinsic or associated kinases, or triggering proteolysis/protein interactions that activate transcription factors. [16][17]

| Receptor class | Core mechanism | High-yield example |
|---|---|---|
| Receptor tyrosine kinase | Ligand -> dimerization -> autophosphorylation | EGFR, PDGFR, insulin receptor |
| Nonreceptor tyrosine kinase-associated receptor | Receptor recruits intracellular kinase | SRC-family kinase signaling |
| GPCR | GDP -> GTP exchange on G protein | cAMP and IP3 signaling |
| Nuclear receptor | Ligand-receptor complex binds DNA | Steroid hormone receptor |
| Notch | Ligand-induced receptor cleavage | Intracellular Notch -> nucleus |
| Wnt/Frizzled | Stabilizes beta-catenin | Beta-catenin enters nucleus |

This receptor summary is based on Figure 1.12 and the receptor classification text. [16][17][18]

### Signal transduction principles

Signal transduction is not a single straight line. Phosphorylation, adaptor proteins, SH2/SH3-domain interactions, feedback loops, proteolysis, protein degradation, and cytoskeletal changes all combine into signaling "nodes" and "hubs." Systems biology is the attempt to understand this higher-order network behavior. [17][18]

### Transcription factors

Most signaling pathways ultimately work by changing gene transcription. Transcription factors require DNA-binding domains plus protein-interaction domains that recruit chromatin modifiers, remodeling complexes, and RNA polymerase. Enhancers can regulate genes from a distance by looping back toward promoters. [19]

### Growth factors

Growth factors do more than increase proliferation; they also influence survival, migration, differentiation, ECM synthesis, and angiogenesis. Dysregulated growth factor signaling converts proto-oncogenes into oncogenic pathways. [19][20]

| Growth factor | High-yield function | Exam association |
|---|---|---|
| EGF / TGF-alpha | Mitogenic for epithelial cells, fibroblasts, hepatocytes | EGFR/ERBB pathway in cancers |
| HGF (scatter factor) | Mitogenic, motogenic, morphogenic | MET receptor; epithelial repair |
| PDGF | Fibroblast, smooth muscle, endothelial proliferation; chemotaxis | Repair, fibrosis, recruitment |
| VEGF | Angiogenesis and vascular permeability | Hypoxia-driven vessel growth |
| FGF | Fibroblast proliferation, angiogenesis, repair | FGF-2, KGF/FGF-7 |
| TGF-beta | ECM synthesis, fibrosis, anti-inflammatory effects | Smad signaling; scar formation |

This growth-factor table summarizes Table 1.1 and the adjoining text. [19][20][21]

### Very high-yield mechanisms

- RAS activation downstream of receptor tyrosine kinases promotes MAPK and PI3K/Akt/mTOR signaling; delayed GTP hydrolysis keeps RAS active and drives proliferative signaling. [17]
- VEGF is the major driver of angiogenesis and also increases vascular permeability. [20]
- TGF-beta signals through serine/threonine kinase receptors and Smad proteins, promotes ECM production, and suppresses acute inflammation. [20][21]

## 4. Extracellular Matrix

ECM is not passive filler. It provides mechanical support, regulates proliferation, acts as a scaffold for repair, and helps create tissue microenvironments. ECM-cell interactions are therefore central to development, healing, fibrosis, and tumor invasion. [21][22]

### Two major forms

| Form | Where found | Main components |
|---|---|---|
| Interstitial matrix | Between cells in connective tissues and around parenchyma | Fibrillar collagens, elastin, fibronectin, proteoglycans, hyaluronan |
| Basement membrane | Beneath epithelium, endothelium, smooth muscle | Type IV collagen, laminin, proteoglycans |

This distinction comes directly from the ECM overview and Figure 1.14. [22]

### Major components

| Component | Main function | High-yield note |
|---|---|---|
| Fibrillar collagens | Tensile strength | Major connective-tissue scaffold |
| Type IV collagen | Basement membrane support | Nonfibrillar collagen |
| Elastin + fibrillin | Elastic recoil | Fibrillin defect -> Marfan syndrome |
| Proteoglycans + hyaluronan | Compressibility, lubrication, growth factor reservoir | Highly hydrated gel |
| Fibronectin | Connects cells to interstitial ECM | Key in wound healing scaffold |
| Laminin | Major basement membrane glycoprotein | Links cells to type IV collagen and other ECM components |
| Integrins | Cell adhesion receptors linking ECM to cytoskeleton | Bind RGD motif and signal inward |

This component table is synthesized from Figures 1.15 to 1.16 and the ECM text. [22][23][24]

### Key mechanisms

- Collagen strength depends on post-translational processing and cross-linking; impaired collagen maturation causes weak connective tissue and poor wound healing. [23]
- Proteoglycans create a hydrated, compressible matrix and also store growth factors such as FGF and HGF. [23]
- Fibronectin is especially important in wound healing because it provides a scaffold for ECM deposition, angiogenesis, and re-epithelialization. [24]
- Laminin is the dominant adhesive glycoprotein in basement membrane. [24]
- Integrins are alpha-beta heterodimers that physically link ECM to the intracellular cytoskeleton and also transduce signals affecting proliferation, shape, migration, and differentiation. [24]

## 5. Maintaining Cell Populations and Regenerative Medicine

Normal tissues maintain homeostasis through a balance between stem cell input, proliferation, differentiation, and apoptosis. Disturbance at any of these points changes cell population size. [25][27]

### Cell-cycle framework

The cell cycle has G1, S, G2, and M phases, while quiescent cells sit in G0. The G1 restriction point commits the cell to the cycle without further growth factor dependence, and the major checkpoints are G1/S and G2/M. [24][25]

| Feature | High-yield point |
|---|---|
| G0 | Quiescent state |
| G1 | Presynthetic growth |
| S | DNA synthesis |
| G2 | Premitotic growth |
| M | Mitosis |
| Restriction point | Commitment point in late G1 |
| G1/S checkpoint | Checks DNA integrity before replication |
| G2/M checkpoint | Checks successful replication before mitosis |

This table is based on Figure 1.17 and the checkpoint text. [25]

### Cyclins, CDKs, and inhibitors

Cell-cycle progression is driven by cyclin-CDK complexes. Cyclins D, E, A, and B appear sequentially, and CDK activity rises and falls with cyclin abundance. Cyclin D-CDK4/6 and cyclin E-CDK2 help drive the G1 to S transition by phosphorylating Rb. Cyclin A complexes act in S phase, and cyclin B-CDK1 is essential for the G2 to M transition. [25][26]

Checkpoint restraint is provided by CDK inhibitors. One family, p21, p27, and p57, broadly inhibits multiple CDKs. The INK4 family, p15, p16, p18, and p19, selectively inhibits cyclin D-CDK4 and cyclin D-CDK6. If checkpoint control fails, genetically damaged cells continue dividing and accumulate mutations that predispose to malignancy. [25][26]

If DNA damage is too severe to repair, cells undergo senescence or apoptosis, largely through p53-dependent mechanisms. [25]

### Stem cells

Stem cells are defined by self-renewal and by the ability to generate differentiated progeny. Self-renewal may be asymmetric, producing one differentiating daughter and one retained stem cell, or symmetric, expanding the stem-cell pool. [26]

| Stem-cell type | Potency / role | High-yield note |
|---|---|---|
| Embryonic stem cell | Broadest developmental potential | Inner cell mass of blastocyst |
| Tissue (adult) stem cell | Maintains a specific tissue | Restricted differentiation repertoire |
| Hematopoietic stem cell | Replenishes blood lineages | Can repopulate marrow clinically |
| Mesenchymal stem cell | Gives rise to stromal tissues | Bone, cartilage, fat, muscle support lineages |
| Induced pluripotent stem cell | Reprogrammed somatic cell with ES-like properties | Patient-specific regenerative medicine strategy |

This stem-cell table synthesizes Figures 1.19 to 1.22 and the associated text. [26][27][28]

### Stem-cell niches and regenerative medicine

Adult stem cells live in niches that keep them quiescent until expansion is needed. Examples include bone marrow, hair follicle bulge, corneal limbus, intestinal crypts, canals of Hering in the liver, and the subventricular zone in the brain. [27][28]

Regenerative medicine aims to isolate, expand, and transplant stem cells or their progeny to repair damaged tissue. A major conceptual advance is the generation of induced pluripotent stem cells by introducing genes that restore "stem-ness" to differentiated cells, which may reduce rejection because the cells are patient-specific. Practical integration of replacement cells into damaged tissues remains difficult. [28]

## Exam-Focused Takeaways

- If the stem mentions disease susceptibility without a coding mutation, think noncoding DNA or epigenetic regulation first. [2][3]
- If transcription increases, think open chromatin, histone acetylation, and accessible enhancers/promoters. [3][4]
- If a membrane transport defect causes swelling after injury, think ATP depletion and failed ion pumping. [9]
- If the question asks where a misfolded membrane protein is trapped, think rough ER and unfolded protein response. [12]
- If a question asks about degradation of ubiquitin-tagged cytosolic proteins, think proteasome, not lysosome. [13][14]
- If angiogenesis is the issue, VEGF is the default answer unless a more specific context is given. [20]
- If basement membrane integrity is tested, think laminin and type IV collagen. [22][24]
- If a cell-cycle question centers on G1 to S transition, think cyclin D/CDK4-6, cyclin E/CDK2, Rb, and CDK inhibitors. [25][26]
- If a stem-cell question asks about tissue maintenance, think niche-regulated adult stem cells rather than embryonic stem cells. [27][28]
